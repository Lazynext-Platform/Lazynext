## Why Benchmarks Matter

Video editing performance isn't a single number. It's the sum of dozens of interdependent factors: timeline scrubbing responsiveness, effect render time, export throughput, memory pressure under multi-layer compositions, and UI frame rate during real-time playback. Most editor benchmarks focus exclusively on export speed — a metric that matters, but tells you almost nothing about the editing experience itself.

We built a comprehensive benchmarking suite that measures Lazynext across all of these dimensions, then ran the same workloads against DaVinci Resolve 19 and Premiere Pro 2025 on identical hardware. Here's the methodology, the numbers, and what we learned.

## Methodology and Test Hardware

All benchmarks ran on a single workstation: AMD Ryzen 9 7950X (16 cores, 32 threads), 64 GB DDR5-6000, NVIDIA RTX 4090 (24 GB VRAM), Samsung 990 Pro 2 TB NVMe SSD. Tests were repeated five times; we report the median with 95th percentile variance. The test composition consists of a 10-minute timeline at 4K 60fps with eight video tracks, four audio tracks, twelve LUT-based color grades, eight Gaussian blur instances, and four simultaneous picture-in-picture transforms. Source media: Apple ProRes 422 HQ.

We measure three categories: **scrubbing latency** (time from playhead move to frame display), **effect render throughput** (frames per second for GPU-accelerated effects), and **export wall-clock time** (H.265 4K 60fps at 80 Mbps target). We also track peak memory consumption and sustained GPU utilization throughout each benchmark.

## Results: Scrub Latency and Timeline Responsiveness

Scrubbing latency is arguably the most important metric for editors — a sluggish timeline breaks creative flow faster than any other performance issue. Lazynext achieves a median scrub-to-display latency of **8.2 ms** at 4K, compared to 14.7 ms for DaVinci Resolve 19 and 22.3 ms for Premiere Pro 2025. The gap widens under load: with all effects enabled and eight simultaneous video tracks, Lazynext stays at 11.4 ms while Resolve climbs to 31.2 ms and Premiere Pro reaches 48.9 ms.

This advantage comes from two architectural decisions. First, Lazynext's compositor runs entirely on the GPU through wgpu, avoiding CPU-GPU round-trips during scrubbing. Second, our CRDT-based state model means every timeline element carries its own cached render representation — when you scrub, we don't recalculate the scene graph from scratch. We only invalidate and recompute the elements that changed.

## Results: Effect Render Throughput

For GPU-accelerated effects, we measured sustained frames-per-second during timeline playback with all effects active. Lazynext maintains **58.7 fps** (capped at display refresh), dropping to 54.2 fps with 10+ effect instances. DaVinci Resolve 19 achieves 59.1 fps but shows higher variance under multi-track compositions. Premiere Pro 2025 delivers 42.3 fps with the full effect stack, occasionally dropping below 30 fps during complex keyframe transitions.

Lazynext's 18 blend modes, SDF-based masking, and WebGPU compute shaders all target data-parallel execution. Our wgpu scope analyzer hoists uniform bindings and eliminates redundant pipeline switches, which keeps draw calls significantly lower than traditional immediate-mode rendering approaches. For effects like JFA signed distance field masks, Lazynext is consistently 2-3x faster than CPU-bound implementations because the entire distance field computation stays on the GPU.

## Results: Export Performance and Memory

Export is where raw throughput matters most. Lazynext completes the 10-minute 4K H.265 export in **4 minutes 12 seconds** (median), compared to 3 minutes 58 seconds for DaVinci Resolve 19 and 7 minutes 44 seconds for Premiere Pro 2025. Resolve edges ahead on pure encoding speed due to its deeper NVENC integration, but the gap narrows as effect complexity increases — Lazynext's GPU-resident effect pipeline means rendering effects during export doesn't require a context switch.

Peak memory consumption tells an even more dramatic story. Lazynext uses **3.8 GB RSS** during the full benchmark suite, while DaVinci Resolve uses 8.2 GB and Premiere Pro uses 11.4 GB. Our WASM binary (17 MB) is smaller than a single frame of ProRes 422 HQ at 4K. The compositor allocates GPU buffers once and reuses them across frames; the CRDT state model avoids duplicating timeline data; and Rust's ownership system eliminates the garbage collection pauses that cause stutter in managed-language editors.

## What These Numbers Mean for Editors

The takeaway isn't that Lazynext is "faster" in every dimension — Resolve's export pipeline is genuinely impressive, and there are specific workloads where a purpose-built native encoder wins. The takeaway is that a browser-based editor, running on WebAssembly and WebGPU, can now match or exceed the interactive performance of native desktop applications. The abstraction tax that historically made web-based creative tools feel sluggish is no longer structural — it's a question of engineering quality.

Our benchmarking suite is open source and runs as part of our CI pipeline. Every commit is measured, every regression is caught before it ships. We'll publish updated numbers as the engine evolves.
